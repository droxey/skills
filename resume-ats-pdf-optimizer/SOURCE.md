# Extracted Findings (for skill design)

## Core claim corrections

- The widely repeated "75% of resumes are rejected by ATS" claim is presented as unsupported.
- Source attributes the claim to Preptel (2012), noting the company shut down in 2013 without published methodology.
- Source claims zero peer-reviewed studies support that exact 75% figure.

## ATS behavior context

- 98% of Fortune 500 companies use an ATS.
- 92% of ATS configurations in a cited 2025 recruiter/platform study reportedly do **not** auto-reject based on resume content; they rank/sort.
- Knockout questions (work authorization, years of experience, etc.) are described as major pre-human gates.
- Formatting/parsing failures are cited as 23% of rejections.
- Qualification gaps are cited as 57% of rejections.

## ResumeAdapter pipeline baseline (Q1 2026)

- Median first-submission ATS score: **48/100**.
- 51% of resumes score below 50.
- 42% score below 40.
- 23% score 80 or above.
- 52% of target JD keywords are missing on average.
- 99% of resumes have experience-section gaps flagged.
- 94% have skills-section gaps flagged.
- 93% have both skills and experience flagged.

## Finding 01 — score distribution

- More than half of first submissions are below the midpoint (50/100).
- Sub-40 resumes are framed as likely to be deprioritized.
- 70+ is framed as a useful practical threshold (top ~41% in this dataset).

## Finding 02 — keyword gap

- Literal keyword matching is emphasized over semantic matching.
- Missing keyword average: 52%.
- Qualified candidates can still underperform if they do not use JD-exact vocabulary.

## Finding 03 — top failure section

- Experience section is the #1 failure point (99% flagged).
- Skills section is also frequently flagged (94%).
- Source argues ATS failure is usually a content-language alignment problem, not only formatting.

## Finding 04 — optimization impact

- One optimization cycle yields median +13 points and mean +17 points.
- Maximum reported improvement: +87 points.
- Largest gains are reported for low starting scores (20-40 band).

## Finding 05 — industry variance

- Median first-submission scores differ by industry.
- Reported low medians: sales (24), healthcare/nursing (28), digital marketing (28).
- Higher medians reported: finance (43), executive (45).

## Human review funnel context

- 250 applications per corporate posting (cited aggregated sources).
- Recruiter initial scan time: 7.4 seconds median (TheLadders study).
- 4-6 candidates interviewed per open role.
- Recruiters handling 20-40 open requisitions.
- Practical implication in source: ranking position strongly affects visibility.

## ATS platform mix context (cited in source)

- Workday: ~39% of Fortune 500 usage.
- SAP SuccessFactors: ~13%.
- iCIMS: ~7%.
- Oracle Taleo: ~5%.
- Source suggests formatting strictness varies by platform and employer configuration.

## Third-party statistics cited in source

- 88% of employers acknowledge qualified candidates can be screened out by automated criteria mismatch (HBS/Accenture hidden workers framing).
- 27M U.S. workers estimated as "hidden workers" in that framework.
- Including exact job title in resume associated with 10.6x interview-likelihood increase in cited Jobscan analysis.
- Tailoring resume per posting associated with 30% callback increase in cited Jobscan analysis.
- Recruiter ATS usage trend cited as increasing (e.g., 93% figure in 2026 citation chain).
- Multi-column layout parsing accuracy cited as materially lower than single-column in one cited statistic.
- Application volume per role increased significantly in cited Ashby trend report window.

## Methodology notes from source

- Data described as anonymized aggregate ATS scoring analyses through Q1 2026.
- Premium analyses include keyword and section-level gap data.
- Source explicitly says it does not measure universal employer-level ATS rejection rates.
- Source positions ATS score as compatibility proxy, not universal hiring threshold.
- Source claims CC BY 4.0 reuse for the page’s original statistics with attribution.

## Skill-design implications derived from source

1. Prioritize **experience bullet alignment** first in optimization workflow.
2. Use **exact JD keyword coverage** as a primary score driver.
3. Treat formatting checks as necessary but secondary to content alignment.
4. Report score improvements as probabilistic lift, not guaranteed pass/fail.
5. Include iterative re-score loop after each optimization cycle.
